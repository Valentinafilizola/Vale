Brief Overview

Calm technology for focus and motivation

TerraSync is a dome-shaped ambient desk lamp inspired by a terrarium: soft, living, and quietly responsive. Instead of demanding attention like most productivity tools, it stays peripheral—supporting focus through light, subtle feedback, and a gentle sense of progress.

The object pairs a physical environment with a synchronized Unity “ecosystem” on the user’s laptop. Ambient light in the room influences both the lamp’s brightness and the terrarium’s day/night state, creating a cohesive relationship between the workspace and the digital world.

TerraSync also introduces a focus interaction: a single side button begins a growth cycle in the digital terrarium, visually representing sustained focus. Pausing requires confirmation to avoid accidental interruption—if the user confirms a pause, the plant withers, creating a soft but clear consequence that encourages intentional breaks.

Designed to belong on a desk.
A compact, integrated form—translucent dome for diffusion and an opaque base to hide electronics—so the prototype reads like a product, not exposed wiring.

The Problem

Most focus tools compete for attention

Timers, notifications, and “productivity dashboards” often pull users out of flow. They treat focus as something to track and optimize, rather than something to nurture. I wanted a system that supports focus without becoming another distraction.

I also wanted the physical environment to matter. Lighting and atmosphere affect motivation—but most tools ignore the workspace and live only on a screen. TerraSync reframes focus as an ecosystem: shaped by the room, reinforced through gentle cues, and guided by intentional interactions.

The Solution

A synchronized physical + digital ecosystem

TerraSync combines three elements into one coherent experience:

1) Ambient adaptation: a photoresistor senses room brightness, and the lamp responds with a soft, diffused glow that fits the environment.
2) Digital synchronization: the Arduino sends sensor data to Unity via serial communication, mapping the workspace into a terrarium simulation (day/night shifts based on real light or time).
3) Intentional focus interaction: a side-mounted button toggles focus states that drive plant growth, pause requests, and withering—making breaks deliberate and visible.

Value Proposition

Make focus feel cultivated, not measured

TerraSync is designed around a simple belief: focus improves when progress is felt, not forced. Instead of alerts and streaks, it uses a living metaphor—growth, light, and ecosystem states—to encourage sustained attention.

Every design decision mapped to three questions:
• Is this calm enough to stay in the background?
• Does it reward focus without pressuring the user?
• Does it make breaks more intentional?
Value proposition

Support deep work through ambient, environment-aware feedback—so focus feels like something you grow, not something you chase.

Form & Visual Direction

Making the prototype read like a product

TerraSync’s form is intentionally minimal: a translucent dome to diffuse RGB light and a clean base that hides the electronics. The goal is a cohesive desk object—quiet, warm, and visually consistent with a focused workspace.

On the screen, the Unity terrarium mirrors the same tone: subtle motion, restrained color, and a “widget-sized” footprint to stay peripheral.

Quiet aesthetics, intentional integration.
A translucent dome softens light output while the base integrates the button and photoresistor into the enclosure for a clean build.

Prototype Walkthrough

How TerraSync behaves

One interaction, clear states.
A single press begins Focus Mode and initiates plant growth in the Unity terrarium. Pressing again triggers a visible pause request (to prevent accidental stopping). A second confirming press finalizes the pause and causes the plant to wither—making breaks feel intentional while keeping the user in control.

System Design

Hardware + software working as one loop

TerraSync is built as a closed loop between the environment, the lamp, and the Unity ecosystem. The photoresistor reads ambient light and maps it to LED brightness. Those sensor values are transmitted over serial to Unity, which updates the terrarium’s day/night state to match the workspace context.

The focus button drives a simple state machine: Idle → Active Focus → Pause Requested → Paused. Growth and withering animations are tied to these states so progress is visible, but never distracting.

Designed as a state machine.
Clear states made the interaction predictable and reduced accidental “breaks” by requiring confirmation before pausing.

Build Details

Materials, components, and constraints

The physical prototype uses an Arduino Uno, RGB LEDs, a photoresistor (voltage divider), a push button, resistors, and wiring. USB provides power and serial communication.

The enclosure is 3D printed in two parts: a translucent dome for diffusion and an opaque base to house and secure electronics. The button and photoresistor are embedded into the base to preserve a clean product form.

Clean assembly for reliability.
Component placement and enclosure constraints were designed around stable sensing, consistent diffusion, and easy access for iteration.

Reflection

Where design and engineering had to meet

The hardest part wasn’t any single component—it was making the entire experience feel coherent. Small decisions (sensor smoothing, threshold mapping, animation pacing, LED diffusion) had outsized impact on whether TerraSync felt calm or chaotic.

I learned that “ambient” is a systems problem: it requires careful calibration so inputs feel stable, outputs feel intentional, and the interface remains peripheral even when it’s dynamic.

Key Learnings

Ambient experiences succeed through restraint

• Stable mapping beats reactive mapping. Smoothing and thresholds improved trust and reduced jitter.
• Interaction clarity matters more than “features.” A simple confirmation step prevented accidental disengagement.
• Product feel is physical and digital. Enclosure details and UI pacing both determine perceived quality.

Next Steps

Refine the ecosystem and validate behavior

Next, I would test TerraSync in real working sessions to validate whether the focus interaction actually reduces accidental context switching. I’d measure time-to-reenter focus after breaks, perceived distraction, and whether the “pause request” step feels helpful or annoying.

On the system side, I’d refine light mapping (auto-calibration per room), expand growth states for longer sessions, and improve the Unity widget’s “peripheral” behavior (subtler motion and clearer state messaging).
