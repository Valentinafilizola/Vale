01 · The problem

Traditional ground control asks operators to monitor everything at once.

Dense panels, competing alerts, and separated displays increase visual search. In a headset, that same density can become more disruptive—not less.

A

Attention is fragmented

Video, telemetry, maps, and checklists compete for the same visual channel.

B

Critical signals blend in

When every panel is visually loud, urgency becomes harder to distinguish.

C

Spatial UI adds constraints

Field of view, arm fatigue, depth, and passthrough safety shape every interaction.

How might we preserve situational awareness while making mission-critical information immediately reachable in mixed reality?

02 · Research

Immersion is promising. Better performance still has to be designed.

Mixed-reality ground stations are already being built and evaluated for video, speech, and teleoperation. The evidence supports feasibility, while the broader advantage over established GCS workflows remains an open design question. I treated MR as a new interaction surface—not an automatic usability upgrade.

Research posture: preserve the proven strengths of familiar GCS layouts, then use spatial computing only where it can reduce search, clarify priority, or support hands-free access.

03 · Design principles

Four rules kept the interface calm under pressure.

01

The primary task never disappears.

The live feed remains the visual anchor while supporting information moves around it.

Grounded in continuous task visibility
02

Related information stays close.

Telemetry, status, and alerts occupy predictable locations to shorten visual travel.

Grounded in gaze-path efficiency
03

Urgency changes behavior, not just color.

Critical alerts move into the primary field and require acknowledgment; routine updates remain peripheral.

Grounded in alert hierarchy
04

Interaction respects the body.

Frequent controls remain near a neutral posture, while large movements are reserved for deliberate workspace changes.

Grounded in fatigue constraints

07 · Validation

Testing attention, not just whether people can click.

The study is scoped for 3–5 participants and evaluates whether users can locate status, respond to warnings, and maintain orientation across the workspace.

Tasks

Find battery status, locate altitude, switch camera, respond to a geofence warning, confirm mission state.

What matters

Completion, time, confusion points, gaze direction, panel movement, and perceived workload.

Study format

Quest sessions where possible, with desktop-simulated walkthroughs clearly labeled when headset access is limited.

08 · Iteration

Every revision should trace back to observed behavior.

Version 1

Telemetry lived in an isolated utility panel.

Observed pattern

Users searched near the map and video before checking the side panel.

Version 2

Core telemetry moved beside the live feed, reducing the visual jump.

09 · Reflection

Spatial computing did not remove complexity. It gave me new ways to organize it.

The project reinforced that safety-critical MR should build on familiar mental models, make priority physically legible, and earn every interruption. Next, I would explore adaptive spatial anchoring and low-effort wrist input as ways to reduce repositioning and arm fatigue.
